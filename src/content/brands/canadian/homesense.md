The store receives new shipments on Monday and Friday mornings, which means frequent visits often yield different selections. Unlike traditional retailers, HomeSense purchases overstock, previous season merchandise, and factory seconds directly from manufacturers, passing those savings to customers without relying on promotional sales or coupon campaigns.

Members can use their STYLE+ card at HomeSense, Winners, and Marshalls interchangeably, and returns are accepted across all three store brands.

HomeSense operates both standalone locations and superstore formats paired with Winners or Marshalls. The superstore design, first introduced in Markham, Ontario in 2003, features open passageways between the two retailers, allowing customers to shop home goods and apparel in one trip. Store associates arrange merchandise throughout the day, and clearance items marked with red stickers appear at the back of aisles and endcaps.

Seasonal sections offer particularly strong value, with holiday décor, patio furniture, and entertaining essentials appearing well ahead of peak demand periods. January and February clearance events provide the steepest discounts as stores make room for spring inventory. HomeSense does not operate an e-commerce platform, so all shopping occurs in physical locations. Most stores coordinate with local delivery services for large furniture purchases, with fees starting around $45 arranged directly between the customer and delivery provider.

The retailer maintains consistent [return policies](https://www.homesense.ca/en/returns) requiring original receipts and attached tags for refunds in original payment form within the applicable timeframe. ## What HomeSense Means to Canadian Shoppers

HomeSense has become a familiar destination for Canadians furnishing apartments, refreshing seasonal décor, or searching for distinctive home accents without department store pricing. The off-price model attracts shoppers who enjoy discovering unexpected items rather than browsing predictable inventory. Home cooks find European cookware brands, apartment dwellers locate accent furniture that fits tight budgets, and gift-givers rely on the constantly changing giftware section for hostess presents and wedding registry alternatives.

The brand operates as part of the TJX Canada family alongside Winners and Marshalls, but focuses exclusively on home categories rather than apparel. This specialization means HomeSense dedicates entire sections to bedding, bath accessories, kitchen gadgets, lighting, rugs, wall art, and storage solutions. Shoppers compare HomeSense to a more curated version of a home goods department store, with merchandise presentation emphasizing discovery over organized product hierarchies.

## Brand History and Entry Into Canada

TJX Companies entered Canada in 1990 by acquiring the five-store Winners chain and expanding it into the country's largest off-price apparel retailer. Building on that foundation, TJX launched HomeSense in 2001 to bring the off-price home fashions concept to Canadian consumers. The brand was modeled after HomeGoods, TJX's established US home store division, but represented the company's first dedicated home goods banner outside the United States.

HomeSense grew steadily through the 2000s, reaching seven locations by 2002 and 101 stores by 2015. The company introduced the superstore format in 2003, pairing HomeSense with Winners locations in a single building connected by interior passageways. The first superstore opened in Markham, Ontario, spanning over 50,000 square feet with dual branding and separate entrances. This format became increasingly common as TJX expanded both brands, offering customers combined shopping experiences in suburban power centers and regional malls.

By September 2025, HomeSense operated 161 stores across Canadian provinces. The brand later expanded internationally to Europe in 2008 and the United States in 2017, but Canada remains its largest market and original home. While TJX Companies is headquartered in Framingham, Massachusetts, HomeSense itself originated on Canadian soil and continues to maintain its strongest presence here.

## Why Canadians Choose HomeSense

The primary draw is pricing structure. HomeSense purchases surplus inventory, discontinued items, and previous season merchandise in bulk directly from manufacturers and importers. This buying strategy allows the retailer to offer recognizable brands at discounts typically ranging from 20 to 60 percent below conventional retail prices. Shoppers encounter brand names like KitchenAid, Calvin Klein Home, Kate Spade, and European importers alongside lesser-known labels, all merchandised without the traditional premium pricing.

The rotating inventory model also appeals to deal-oriented consumers. Because HomeSense buys opportunistically rather than through standing purchase orders, merchandise changes constantly. Regular shoppers develop routines of checking stores weekly or visiting on Monday and Friday mornings when new shipments typically arrive. This treasure-hunt approach contrasts with big-box home stores where inventory remains consistent month to month.

HomeSense also fills a specific niche for Canadians furnishing spaces on tight timelines. University students moving into apartments, new homeowners staging rooms before furniture arrives, and renters refreshing dated spaces all find functional items at accessible price points. Seasonal shoppers appreciate early availability of holiday décor, patio furniture, and entertaining essentials, often appearing in stores weeks before traditional retailers stock equivalent items.

## Pricing, Rewards, and Membership Programs

HomeSense does not typically run promotional sales, distribute coupons, or offer percentage-off events. The business model assumes everyday low pricing eliminates the need for temporary discounts. Clearance markdowns do occur on slow-moving inventory, identified by bright red stickers, with these items typically relocated to aisle ends or dedicated clearance sections at store backs.

The [TJX Canada STYLE+ loyalty program](https://www.tjxstyleplus.ca/) provides extended return windows and contest entries rather than points or cashback rewards. Members receive 30 days to return purchases with receipts instead of the standard 10-day window for non-members. Each purchase with a scanned STYLE+ card automatically enters the member into monthly drawings for $100 e-gift cards redeemable at HomeSense, Winners, or Marshalls. The program charges no membership fee and cards work across all three TJX Canada banners.

Members also receive invitations to exclusive shopping events offering early access to stores during special promotions, though these events generally remain accessible to walk-in customers as well. The primary tangible benefit remains the extended return period, which provides flexibility for home décor purchases that may require measuring, color matching, or partner approval before committing.

Customers using the TJX Rewards credit card earn five percent back in rewards certificates on purchases at HomeSense and sister stores. New cardholders receive a 10 percent discount on their first in-store purchase, though the credit card operates separately from the free STYLE+ membership program.

## Returns, Warranties, and Customer Experience

HomeSense maintains a straightforward [return policy](https://www.homesense.ca/en/returns) requiring merchandise in unused, original condition with tags attached and register receipts presented within the applicable timeframe. Standard returns are accepted within 10 days for refunds in original payment tender. STYLE+ members receive 30 days for returns meeting the same conditions. Pierced jewelry and gift cards cannot be returned under any circumstances.

Returns without receipts require valid government-issued photo identification and result in gift card refunds rather than payment tender refunds. Management reserves the right to limit or decline returns at their discretion, particularly for customers with patterns of excessive returns or missing documentation.

HomeSense returns are accepted at any Canadian location regardless of purchase location. Additionally, sister stores Winners and Marshalls accept HomeSense returns, allowing customers to return items at whichever TJX banner is most convenient. This cross-banner return policy acknowledges the shared ownership and similar operational practices across the three retailers.

Warranties follow manufacturer terms rather than HomeSense-specific coverage. Small appliances, electronics, and cookware sold with manufacturer warranties remain the responsibility of the brand rather than the retailer. Customers experiencing defects or quality issues should retain product packaging and documentation for potential warranty claims.

Customer service inquiries are handled through a toll-free number (1-800-646-9466) operating Monday through Saturday from 9 AM to 5 PM Eastern Time. HomeSense does not maintain live chat, email support, or social media customer service channels, directing most inquiries to the phone line or in-store assistance.

## Online Shopping and Canadian Shipping

HomeSense does not operate an e-commerce website or mobile shopping app. All purchases occur in physical store locations, requiring customers to visit in person to browse inventory and complete transactions. This brick-and-mortar exclusivity stems from the off-price business model, where inventory changes rapidly and quantities remain limited. Operating an online platform would require different supply chain infrastructure and inventory management systems incompatible with the treasure-hunt shopping experience HomeSense provides.

For large furniture pieces, area rugs, or bulky home goods, most HomeSense locations partner with local delivery services to transport purchases to customer homes. Delivery fees begin around $45 and vary based on distance and item size. Customers arrange delivery directly with the third-party service rather than through HomeSense staff, and the retailer does not coordinate or guarantee delivery timelines. Shoppers transporting items personally should note that HomeSense does not offer hold services, so items must be purchased and removed the same day.

The lack of online shopping means rural and remote Canadian communities rely on periodic trips to regional centers with HomeSense locations. The brand concentrates stores in urban and suburban areas with sufficient population density to support the off-price model. Small-town shoppers often combine HomeSense visits with other errands during city trips.

## How Canadians Find Deals and Savings

Regular shoppers develop strategies for maximizing value at HomeSense. Visiting on Monday and Friday mornings increases chances of accessing newly arrived merchandise before popular items sell out. Checking the back of each aisle reveals clearance items marked with red stickers, as stores consolidate discounted merchandise in less prominent locations. Seasonal sections offer particularly strong value when shopped ahead of peak demand, such as purchasing patio furniture in early spring or holiday décor in October.

January and February represent prime clearance periods as stores liquidate winter and holiday inventory to accommodate spring merchandise. These months often feature the deepest discounts on bedding, kitchen items, and home accents. Conversely, shopping immediately before major holidays may result in picked-over inventory, as popular seasonal items sell quickly without replenishment.

Savvy shoppers also visit Winners and Marshalls locations during the same trip, as all three TJX Canada banners occasionally stock overlapping home goods. Comparing inventory across sister stores within the same shopping center sometimes reveals different selections or pricing on similar items. The shared ownership means merchandise allocation varies by location based on local demographics and buying patterns.

HomeSense does not price match competitors or honor competitor coupons, as the off-price model already assumes discounted pricing. However, the absence of manufacturer-advertised pricing means shoppers should research typical retail costs for specific brands and products to verify actual value. Some items represent genuine bargains while others may be priced closer to conventional retail than the marketing suggests.
